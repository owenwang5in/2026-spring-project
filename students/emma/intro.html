Abstract


Following the implementation of the "no-kill" policy in Taiwan, public animal shelters have faced a significant increase in dog numbers. Despite the growing number of dogs awaiting homes, adoption rates are still low, creating a need to understand the barriers to pet adoption.

This study investigates the factors causing people to hesitate when considering dog adoption, with a specific focus on how expected veterinary medical costs influence their final decisions.
This study implements a four-stage research design, combining field observations with a convenience sampling questionnaire survey of the general public (N = 60). Data were collected through standardized on-site interview protocols and analyzed using descriptive statistics, data visualization, and Chi-square tests to examine behavioral relationships.

We found that financial burden and limited living space were the main barriers to dog adoption. More than half of the respondents said they would be more willing to adopt a dog if the medical cost decreases.

Our finding were that medical fees really did effect people willingness of adopting a dog they impact the bigger picture by showing us that if we lower the medical fees than more people will be more willing to adopt a dog and that can increase the rate of adoption


Introduction


We believe that lowering pet healthcare costs can increase people’s willingness to adopt dogs, and our Smallest Actionable Point (SAP) is to test if reducing medical costs will change people’s decisions. Many stray dogs like AoAo need homes; he was born as a stray and, although he needs to be alone with other dogs, he is friendly, playful, and loves water and toys. However, many people do not adopt because they worry about high medical costs like vet visits and vaccines. Our goal is to increase dog adoption by reducing this financial burden. To learn this, we will use a survey to ask people about their willingness to adopt, their reasons for not adopting, and if lower medical costs would make them more willing to adopt.


Predictions

For the first question, we predicted that most people had never owned a dog. For the second question, we thought that most people would believe the average yearly medical cost of owning a dog is more than 28,000 NTD. For the third question, we predicted that financial barriers would be the main reason preventing people from adopting a dog. We also believed that most people would take a dog to the veterinarian immediately if it became sick or injured. Finally, we thought that people would choose numbers above 3, indicating that lower medical fees in the future would increase their willingness to adopt a dog. After analyzing the survey results, we found that some of our predictions were correct, while others surprised us. Comparing our predictions with the actual results helped us better understand people’s attitudes toward dog ownership and the factors that influence adoption decisions.
