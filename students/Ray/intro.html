Abstract

Following the implementation of the "no-kill" policy in Taiwan, public animal shelters have faced a significant increase in dog numbers. Despite the growing number of dogs awaiting homes, adoption rates are still low, creating a need to understand the barriers to pet adoption.

This study investigates the factors causing people to hesitate when considering dog adoption, with a specific focus on how expected veterinary medical costs influence their final decisions.

This study implements a four-stage research design, combining field observations with a convenience sampling questionnaire survey of the general public (N = 60). Data were collected through standardized on-site interview protocols and analyzed using descriptive statistics, data visualization, and Chi-square tests to examine behavioral relationships.

We found that financial burden and limited living space were the main barriers to dog adoption. More than half of the respondents said they would be more willing to adopt a dog if the medical cost decreases.

Our findings were that medical fees really did affect people's willingness to adopt a dog. They impact the bigger picture by showing us that if we lower the medical fees, then more people will be more willing to adopt a dog, which can increase the rate of adoption.

Introduction

In 2017, Taiwan became the second country in Asia to implement a "Zero Euthanasia" policy for stray animals in public shelters. While this milestone reflected an advancement in animal rights, it also brought unprecedented challenges to urban governance. Without the option of euthanasia to control shelter populations, public facilities quickly reached a state of overcapacity, leading to a decline in animal living standards and increased pressure on management resources.

After the post-zero euthanasia policy was implemented, the focus of animal welfare has shifted from preventing death to promoting life, making the improvement of adoption rates the most critical solution to the shelter crisis. However, a gap appears between public sympathy for stray dogs and actual adoption intentions, indicating that obstacles still prevent these animals from being adopted.

While the public generally agrees with the value of "adopt, don't shop," potential adopters often face limits in reality during the decision making process. Besides common factors such as living space and time allocation, assessing financial capacity is a key hurdle in determining adoption willingness. Compared to the initial cost of acquiring a dog, the recurring expenses during the long-term care process, especially the highly uncertain medical costs, often deter adopters.

In promoting stray dog adoption, apart from advocating animal welfare concepts, understanding the decision-making obstacles of potential owners is crucial. Although the current shelter system strives to increase the matching rate, financial burden is always a key variable that influences adoption intentions. This study aims to systematically collect data on public experience with dog ownership, perceptions of medical expenses, and coping behaviors through a survey method. Through quantitative analysis, we attempt to clarify whether there is a gap between public expectations and reality regarding medical costs, and how changes in medical costs substantially affect their adoption decisions.

This study aims to increase dog adoption rates and reduce the risk of dog returns by lowering the psychological and economic barriers to dog health care for owners. To demonstrate this argument, this study proposes a minimum feasible action point: reducing the fear of unknown medical costs among potential owners through precise medical budgeting, education, and support mechanisms. This action point is connected to the long term goal of this study: when we can accurately grasp the public's ability to afford medical expenses and their coping logic, animal protection organizations can design targeted adoption medical insurance or medical subsidy programs to increase people's desire to adopt and achieve the goal of increasing the adoption rate of dogs.

Predictions

Before the data analysis, several hypotheses were formulated based on the research objectives and relevant literature. Regarding the research hypotheses, it was initially predicted that the majority of participants had never owned a dog. For the secondary variable, based on statistics from the Ministry of Agriculture, R.O.C., which report the average annual medical cost of dog ownership to be 28,000 NTD, we hypothesized that most respondents would estimate the cost to exceed this number. Additionally, financial constraints were anticipated to be the main barrier to dog adoption. Regarding medical care, we expected that most individuals would take the dog to a veterinarian immediately if it became sick or injured. Lastly, participants were projected to score above 3 on the Likert scale, indicating that reduced medical fees would positively influence their willingness to adopt.
