1. Characteristics of the Sample: This section explains the respondents of who answered the survey.

1.1 Total Sample Size: A total of 60 respondents successfully completed the survey and provided usable responses.

1.2 Gender

- I chose a pie chart because it shows the percentage of people in each gender category and makes the data easy to understand.

- The gender distribution of respondents was balanced. The pie chart was used because it clearly shows the proportion of respondents in each category and it makes the results easy to understand.

- The gender data shows that the survey responses were almost evenly split between males and females, with both groups making up 48.3% of respondents. This means that the survey results are unlikely to be influenced by one of the gender, making the findings more balanced. Only a small amount of people selected "Other" or preferred not to disclose their gender.

1.3 Age Distribution

- I chose bar graphs because for me, I think bar graphs are the easiest to look at and easy to compare the frequencies of the different gender categories and clearly shows which category has the highest and lowest number of responses.

- The bar graph was used because it allows for easy comparison of the frequencies across different age groups and clearly shows which groups had the highest and lowest numbers of respondents.

- The age distribution shows that respondents came from a wide range of age groups, most of the people are aged 18–24 and 35–44. This suggests that the survey reflects the opinions of both younger adults and middle-aged adults. Since the only a few people are over 65 years old, the results may not fully represent the views of older adults.

2. Survey data analysis
Q1:

The first survey question was Have you ever owned a dog? The goal of this was to find out whether people have experience owning dogs and how common dog ownership is. This is a single-choice question that collects categorical data. I use a bar graph for this because it’s easy to compare different categories, most and least common responses. The most common response was “ No, never owned a dog” and the other two had the same amount of responses.

Q2:

This question was: How much do you think the average annual medical expenses per year for owning a dog would be? The reason we asked this was to find out how well people know the medical care costs of owning a dog. This is an open-ended question that collects numerical data. To analyze the responses, the answers were grouped by value, the frequency of each amount was counted, and the results were converted into percentages. A line graph is used here because it makes it easy to see the overall pattern of responses and compare the frequencies of different expense estimates. It also helps show how the values change across the data. The most common answer was chosen by 16.7% of respondents, while the least common was chosen by 1.7% of respondents. Our hypothesis is that most people would think the average yearly medical cost of owning a dog is between 5000 – 10000 NTD.

Q3:

The survey question was, “What do you think is the main reason preventing people from adopting a dog?” The goal was to find out why people don’t adopt dogs. This is a multiple-selection question that collects categorical data. A bar graph was used because it makes the responses easy to compare. The most selected reason was 50%, while the least selected was 3%. The results supported our hypothesis that financial burden and limited living space are the main reasons people don’t adopt dogs.

Q4:

The survey question was, “What will you do if your dog has unusual behavior (such as loss of appetite or vomiting)?” The goal was to find out how people would respond if their dog showed abnormal behavior. This is a single-choice question that collects categorical data. A bar graph was used because it makes the responses easy to compare. The most common response was 48.33%, while the least common was 10%. The results matched our hypothesis that most people would take their dog to the vet immediately.

Q5:

The survey question was, “If the cost of medical care for dogs decreases in the future, how will this affect your willingness to adopt a dog?” The goal was to find out whether lower medical costs would increase people’s willingness to adopt a dog. This is a single-choice question that collects numerical data (1–5 scale). A bar graph was used because it clearly compares the responses. The most common rating was 40%, while the least common was 1.67%. Surprisingly, the results showed that lower medical costs would not greatly increase most people’s willingness to adopt a dog.
