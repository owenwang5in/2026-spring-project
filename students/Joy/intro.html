Introduction

Abstract: Following the implementation of the "no-kill" policy in Taiwan, public animal shelters have faced a significant increase in dog numbers. Despite the growing number of dogs awaiting homes, adoption rates are still low, creating a need to understand the barriers to pet adoption. This study investigates the factors causing people to hesitate when considering dog adoption, with a specific focus on how expected veterinary medical costs influence their final decisions. This study implements a four-stage research design, combining field observations with a convenience sampling questionnaire survey of the general public (N = 60). Data were collected through standardized on-site interview protocols and analyzed using descriptive statistics, data visualization, and Chi-square tests to examine behavioral relationships. We found that financial burden and limited living space were the main barriers to dog adoption. More than half of the respondents said they would be more willing to adopt a dog if the medical cost decreases. Our finding were that medical fees really did effect people willingness of adopting a dog they impact the bigger picture by showing us that if we lower the medical fees than more people will be more willing to adopt a dog and that can increase the rate of adoption.

Introduction: Pet ownership can be rewarding, but many people are discouraged from adopting animals due to concerns about the high cost of care, especially unexpected medical expenses. This research looks at how people feel about the financial side of pet ownership and whether learning about pet illnesses and pet insurance can change their attitudes. Even though pet insurance exists, not many people use it, which may be due to a lack of awareness. This study will use a survey to see if more knowledge can reduce cost concerns, increase interest in pet insurance, and encourage more people to adopt pets.
